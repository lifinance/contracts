LI.FI Programmable Vault Wrapper

A visual walkthrough of what the wrapper does, why we're building it, and how funds flow through it.
Background and how to read this document

What we're building

The Programmable Vault Wrapper is a new product in LI.FI's Earn vertical. The high-level idea: integrators (fintechs, exchanges, wallets — think Coinbase Earn) want to offer their users yield products backed by existing on-chain yield sources (MetaMorpho, Yearn V3, Aave static aTokens, etc.) — but with extra features layered on top that those underlying vaults don't provide themselves.

What integrators wantWhy
A configurable fee suiteFour independent fee types — performance, management, deposit, withdrawal — each optional, each within an immutable cap, each with its own LI.FI/integrator revenue split.
Access controlWhitelist (KYB gate), blacklist (deny list), or both — plus an optional sanctions oracle (Chainalysis) for real-time OFAC screening.
Pause controlsPer-instance deposit pause, plus a factory-wide circuit-breaker LI.FI can trip across every instance at once. Withdrawals are never pausable.
Referrer attributionDeposits can carry a referrer id to credit partners who drive volume.
Custom branding"Coinbase Earn USDC" rather than a generic underlying vault name.

The wrapper is a thin contract that sits between users and an existing yield source, layers these features on, and otherwise stays out of the way. Yield generation itself doesn't change — the underlying vault keeps doing what it was doing; we just observe its growth and apply a fee to it.

Primer: ERC-4626, PPS, and totalAssets()
Primer

What is an ERC-4626 vault?

A standardised "yield-bearing pool token." A user deposits a single ERC-20 asset; the vault mints share tokens representing pro-rata ownership of the pool. As the pool earns yield, each share is redeemable for more of the underlying asset than was deposited.

// the six functions every ERC-4626 vault must expose asset() → IERC20 // the underlying ERC-20 (e.g. USDC) totalAssets() → uint256 // total managed value, denominated in asset() convertToShares(assets) → uint256 // assets × supply / totalAssets convertToAssets(shares) → uint256 // shares × totalAssets / supply deposit(assets, receiver) → shares // pull asset(), mint shares to receiver withdraw(assets, receiver, owner) → shares // burn owner's shares, send asset() to receiver // plus the corresponding preview*() views, max*() limits, and mint()/redeem() variants
Why both the underlying AND our wrapper are ERC-4626
LayerWhy 4626?
MetaMorpho (underlying)It's a curated yield vault — pools USDC from many users, lends it via Morpho Blue markets, distributes yield via PPS appreciation. The 4626 standard is the natural fit for this product.
LI.FI WrapperWe want users (and integrators' apps) to interact with our wrapper the same way they'd interact with MetaMorpho directly — same deposit / withdraw / balanceOf interface, but with our fees, KYC, sanctions, pause primitives layered on top. 4626 conformance means any DeFi-aware app that integrates MetaMorpho can integrate us with one address change.
Primer

What is PPS (price per share)?

The most important number in any 4626 vault. PPS answers "how many units of asset() is one share worth right now?"

// definition — derivable from any 4626 vault PPS = totalAssets() / totalSupply() // units: asset per share // equivalent reads PPS = convertToAssets(1e18) // "value of 1 share (in 18-dec units)" PPS = previewRedeem(1e18) // includes pending fee dilution if any // life of a PPS: // Day 0 — vault deploys empty: PPS = 1.0 (by convention; 1 USDC in → 1 share) // Day 7 — yield accrues: PPS = 1.001 (each share now worth 1.001 USDC) // Day 365 at 5% APY: PPS = 1.05 (5% growth from baseline) // On a yield-fee event: PPS dips slightly (we'll see how, later)
Key intuition: PPS rises with yield and stays flat without it. It only drops in two narrow cases: (a) the underlying lost value (bad debt, smart-contract loss — abnormal); (b) the vault mints new shares to itself as a fee — supply grows while totalAssets stays the same, so each existing share is worth a fraction less. We'll explain the second case in the Architecture section. From here on we'll abbreviate the two PPSes we care about: PPS_under for the underlying vault, PPS_wrap for our wrapper.
The underlying yield source we'll wrap (MetaMorpho on Morpho)

The wrapper itself doesn't generate any yield — it just observes yield generated by whatever ERC-4626 vault it wraps. To make the rest of this document concrete, we need to pick a specific underlying. Throughout the document we use MetaMorpho USDC on Ethereum — a real production vault, ~$50M TVL at time of writing, suitable for regulated-partner products. This section explains what it is and why its yield is observable in the way our wrapper requires.

What is Morpho? What is MetaMorpho?

Morpho is a lending protocol on Ethereum (and a handful of L2s). It has two layers, and our wrapper sits on top of the upper one:

LayerWhat it does
Morpho Blue (the bottom layer)A minimal, immutable lending primitive. Each "market" is a single pair: one asset to borrow against another (e.g. USDC borrowed against wstETH collateral). Borrowers post collateral and take USDC loans; the interest they pay accrues to the lenders supplying that USDC. Hundreds of markets exist independently.
MetaMorpho (the upper layer, where our wrapper attaches)An ERC-4626 vault that pools USDC from many lenders and distributes it across multiple Morpho Blue markets according to a curator's allocation strategy. Each MetaMorpho vault has one asset (e.g. USDC) and one curator (e.g. Gauntlet, Steakhouse). Users see a single yield-bearing position; the curator manages the underlying market allocation behind the scenes.
Why MetaMorpho specifically: it's ERC-4626, has live PPS appreciation with no harvest call required, has a large pool of curators offering yield on stables / ETH / BTC pairs, and is widely used in regulated-partner integrations. The same wrapper design works for any underlying that meets those criteria (Yearn V3 vaults, Aave static aTokens, Sky / Spark sUSDS, etc.).

How yield reaches us, layer by layer

Borrowers in Morpho Blue markets pay interest each block. That interest grows the value of MetaMorpho's holdings. Our wrapper reads MetaMorpho's value via a single live function call. No "report this period" step exists anywhere in the chain — it's just on-chain math evaluated on demand.

Real-world APYs on these vaults are typically 3–8% per year — yield drips in per block, not in discrete chunks.
The wrapper's job — what we add on top

We've covered ERC-4626 in general and we've seen how MetaMorpho generates yield. So why build another vault on top of an existing one? The wrapper exists to let an integrator (e.g. a fintech, exchange, or wallet) plug into MetaMorpho's yield with extra features that MetaMorpho itself doesn't offer:

Critical: our wrapper's asset() is USDC (the raw ERC-20), not Morpho-USDC shares. Users deposit USDC and withdraw USDC. The wrapper internally converts USDC ↔ Morpho shares. If we made asset() = Morpho-shares, totalAssets() would count shares (a number that doesn't change as yield accrues) instead of USDC value (which does). Yield would be invisible to us, and the performance fee we charge later would always compute to zero.

How our wrapper implements totalAssets()

Now that we have three layers, here's the bridge between "we hold MetaMorpho shares" and "we report USDC value to users." A single one-line implementation makes everything downstream work.

// WrapperVault.sol function totalAssets() public view override returns (uint256) { return underlying.convertToAssets(underlying.balanceOf(address(this))); // ^ ^ // MetaMorpho's math our mUSDC balance // // In English: "ask MetaMorpho how many USDC our mUSDC are worth right now" }
Properties of this implementation
PropertyWhy it matters
No caching, no internal counterAlways returns the latest USDC value — yield drips in continuously via MetaMorpho's own PPS appreciation, and we observe it on every read.
Read-only, no state writesCheap to call. ERC-4626's previewRedeem, previewDeposit, etc. can all use this directly.
Single-source-of-truth: the underlyingWe never get out of sync with MetaMorpho. If they take a curator fee, we see post-fee value. If they suffer bad debt, we see the loss immediately.
Compatibility requirement: this only works because the underlying ERC-4626's convertToAssets() returns a continuously-accruing value — yield is already priced in at every block. If the underlying needed an explicit "report this period's yield" call to update its own PPS, our totalAssets() would be stale between those calls. Vetting underlyings for continuous accrual is therefore a hard prerequisite to adding them to the wrapper's allowlist.
Architecture: three independent steps

Older yield vaults (Yearn V1/V2) used a single harvest() function that both realised pending yield and charged a fee on it in one discrete on-chain event — that's where most "harvest" references in DeFi come from. Our wrapper deliberately splits this into three independent steps, each with a different trigger. The word "harvest" doesn't appear in our codebase at all.

1
Yield Realization
Lives in the underlying ERC-4626's PPS. We just observe it via totalAssets(). Alice can call previewRedeem(myShares) any time and see the up-to-date USDC value of her position — no transaction, no gas, no state change.
Trigger: automatic (off-chain to us)
Gas: 0 (no wrapper code runs)
Visible: immediately via any read
2
Fee Accrual
_accrueFee() fires before any state-changing user action (deposit/withdraw/mint/redeem/transfer). It mints new shares to the wrapper itself ("fee-shares") proportional to the yield that accrued since the last call. _lastTotalAssets is the checkpoint we compare against. This is why accrual is effectively "continuous" — any user touching the contract pays the fee on whatever yield has accumulated since the previous touch, so the wrapper's PPS is always truthful at the moment of any user action.
Trigger: every state-changing user action
Gas: ~10–15k overhead
Payer: user (rounding error vs fee captured)
3
Fee Payout (sweep)
sweep() burns the wrapper's accrued fee-shares, redeems the equivalent assets from the underlying, and sends each fee type's amount to the LI.FI treasury and the integrator's 1..5 receiver wallets, per that fee type's own split. No external dispatcher and no caller reward — funds only ever go to the pre-configured receivers.
Trigger: permissionless (anyone can call)
Gas: ~80k
Payer: caller (no reward; payouts go to configured receivers)
Key insight: step 1 has no on-chain step in our wrapper — it's already done by the underlying. Step 2 happens on every user touch (predictable gas, always-truthful PPS). Step 3 is only cashflow — affects no user's economic balance, just converts the wrapper's own fee-shares into assets for the LI.FI treasury and the integrator's receiver wallets.

Which fees flow where: the performance and management fees accrue as fee-shares in step 2 (share-side dilution). The deposit and withdrawal fees are taken asset-side at the moment of the deposit/withdraw — they never move share price. This walkthrough follows the performance fee; the other three use the same plumbing.

Setup: integrator deploys a wrapper instance

Before any user can deposit, the integrator (e.g. Coinbase Earn) deploys a wrapper instance via the LI.FI VaultFactory. One instance per (integrator, underlying, asset) triple. Two deployment techniques are used:

  • BeaconProxy — every wrapper instance is a thin proxy that delegates all calls to one shared implementation contract via a "beacon." Upgrading the beacon's stored implementation atomically upgrades every existing instance (subject to a timelock for safety). One fix, all instances patched.
  • CREATE2 — a deterministic deployment opcode. Given a salt that includes the integrator + underlying + chain ID, the wrapper instance gets the same address on every chain. Nice property for cross-chain UX.

When the underlying ALSO charges its own fee (the common case)
The common case: most production MetaMorpho vaults charge their own curator perf fee (typically 5–15%). Yearn V3 strategies do too. When our wrapper sits on top of one of those, does our perf fee double-charge the user? No. The underlying's fee is already netted out of the PPS that totalAssets() reads — we only see the post-curator-fee yield, and our 10% applies to that net amount. No special handling needed; fees just compose multiplicatively.

APY — what number the user actually sees

Throughout the doc we've talked about yield as something that grows PPS over time. The user-facing version of that is APY — the "X% per year" number in the integrator's app. This section explains what APY is, how it's derived from the data we have, where it should be computed and shown, and how multi-layer fees affect what the user ends up seeing.

What APY (and APR) actually mean

Two related numbers, both annualized:

MetricDefinitionFor our wrapper
APR — Annual Percentage RateSimple annualized rate of return. Ignores compounding within the year.= (PPS_now / PPS_then − 1) × (year / window)
APY — Annual Percentage YieldCompounded annualized rate. Assumes yield is continuously reinvested, which it implicitly is in an ERC-4626 vault (PPS keeps growing).= (PPS_now / PPS_then) ^ (year / window) − 1

For modest yields (single-digit percent), APR and APY differ by less than ~0.2 percentage points. Most user-facing apps display APY; some regulated apps display APR for legal precision.

How APY is derived from on-chain data

An ERC-4626 vault's PPS records the entire yield history — by definition, that's what it tracks. Computing APY requires PPS at two points in time and the elapsed seconds between them.

// pseudo-code, computed off-chain or in an on-chain helper view pps_old = wrapper.convertToAssets(1e18) at block T - window pps_now = wrapper.convertToAssets(1e18) // current elapsed_s = now - T_old // in seconds apr = (pps_now / pps_old - 1) * (365 days / elapsed_s) apy = (pps_now / pps_old) ** (365 days / elapsed_s) - 1
Window choice matters. A 1-day window is noisy (block-level variation can dominate). A 365-day window is too slow to react to rate changes in the underlying. Typical sweet spot is 7 or 30 days — long enough to smooth out noise, short enough to track real changes. The integrator picks the window for their display.

The honesty check — displayed vs. real — lives at the API layer.

Whitelist / KYC gating

Some integrators (regulated fintechs, exchanges) need users to clear an off-chain identity check before depositing. The wrapper supports an optional on-chain whitelist that backs this requirement. When the whitelist is enabled, two things change simultaneously by design:

  • Deposits and mints are gated — only whitelisted addresses can call deposit() / mint().
  • Share transfers are blocked — wshares become non-transferable. Without this, a whitelisted user could simply transfer() their wshares to anyone after the fact, defeating the gate.
Setup

Integrator deploys with whitelist enabled

The integrator's app (Coinbase, etc.) runs the KYC check off-chain. After approval it adds the user's address to an on-chain Whitelist contract referenced by the wrapper. Alice clears KYC; Charlie doesn't.

What this stops: Charlie cannot deposit USDC into the wrapper. Deposit fails before any state change.
Try OTC bypass attempt

Alice tries to OTC her wshares to Charlie — also reverts

Without share-transfer gating, the whitelist would be trivially bypassable: Alice could deposit, then sell her wshares to Charlie off-chain (Venmo, OTC desk) and call vault.transfer(charlie, 100). Charlie would now hold the yield-bearing position without passing KYC. Coupling whitelist-on to non-transferable closes this loophole.

// Wrapper._update() (transfer hook) function _update(address from, address to, uint256 value) internal override { if (whitelistActive() && !isMintOrBurn(from, to)) { revert TransfersDisabled(); } super._update(from, to, value); } // Alice calls transfer(charlie, 100): ↳ revert TransfersDisabled()
Mint/burn still work: the gate fires only on user→user transfers. Internal mints (deposit) and burns (withdraw, fee crystallization) bypass the check because their "other side" is address(0) or the wrapper itself.
Sanctions blacklist + permissionless rescue

Separate from the optional KYC whitelist, the wrapper always carries a blacklist hook for sanctions compliance. The blacklist is normally backed by an OFAC oracle (Chainalysis or similar) plus an internal admin-managed list for edge cases. Two questions need answers when a user gets added mid-position:

  • What stops them from depositing more?
  • What about funds they already have inside? Sanctions compliance means we can't keep accepting their interactions, but we also don't want to permanently freeze their funds — that creates legal and reputational risk.
T+1

Alice has 100 wshares (worth 109 USDC). She gets added to the blacklist.

An admin (or an automated OFAC sync) adds Alice's address to the Blacklist contract. The wrapper reads from this contract on every state-changing call.

// State after blacklist update blacklist.isBlocked(alice) == true alice.balanceOf(wrapper) == 100 wshares (worth ~109 USDC)
Try Alice's actions

All of Alice's own calls now revert

// Wrapper checks blacklist at every entry point function deposit(...) { require(!blacklist.isBlocked(msg.sender)); ↳ revert AddressBlocked } function withdraw(...) { require(!blacklist.isBlocked(msg.sender)); ↳ revert AddressBlocked } function transfer(...) { require(!blacklist.isBlocked(msg.sender)); ↳ revert AddressBlocked }
What this means for Alice: she can no longer move her own funds. If we stopped here, her 109 USDC would be permanently stuck — bad outcome.
Rescue Permissionless

Anyone calls forceWithdraw(alice)

A permissionless rescue function exists specifically for this case. Anyone (the integrator, a watcher script, even Alice's lawyer) can call it. It burns all of Alice's wshares and sends the underlying USDC back to her wallet. The wrapper has now disengaged from her without freezing her funds.

Admin operations: fee changes and pause

Two admin levers worth understanding in detail. Both have a non-obvious property that's important for correctness.

Admin Fee change

Integrator drops the performance fee from 10% → 5%

Shown for the performance fee; the management fee works the same way. The subtle requirement: between the previous fee-accrual checkpoint and the rate change, some yield has accrued at the OLD rate. If we change the rate first and then accrue, the new rate would retroactively apply to that old yield — under- or over-charging depending on direction. The fix is to accrue at the old rate first, then change the rate.

// Wrapper.setFeeRate — one entrypoint for all four fee types function setFeeRate(FeeType t, uint256 newBps) external onlyRole(FEE_MANAGER) { require(newBps <= feeCap[t]); // per-type immutable cap (perf 50%, mgmt 10%, dep/wd 20%) _accrueFee(); // 1) crystallise pending fee at OLD rate (10%) feeRate[t] = newBps; // 2) NOW the rate flips to 5% // 3) subsequent yield accrues at 5% }
Phaseperf rateWhat gets fee'd at this rate
Before call1000 (10%)—
Inside _accrueFee()1000 (10%)All yield since the last accrual
After setFeeRate = 500500 (5%)All yield from this point on
Why the order matters: without the accrue-first step, a fee cut would retroactively apply to historical yield (integrator loses revenue they'd already earned). A fee hike would do the opposite (users get charged retroactively). Either way is unfair and breaks the property that "the fee in effect when the yield happened is what gets charged." This accrue-first rule applies to the share-side fees (performance, management). Deposit and withdrawal fees are charged asset-side at tx time, so a rate change simply applies to future deposits/withdrawals — no retroactive question.
Admin Pause

Pauser halts deposits (withdrawals stay open)

Used when something is wrong with the underlying (e.g. MetaMorpho looks like it might suffer bad debt, or there's a question mark on a downstream Morpho Blue market) and we want to stop new users from entering until things are clarified. Two tiers exist: a per-instance deposit pause (either LI.FI or the integrator may pause and unpause), and a factory-wide circuit-breaker (LI.FI emergency multisig only) that halts deposits across every instance at once for a shared-bytecode emergency. Withdrawals are never pausable by either tier — existing users must always be able to exit.
